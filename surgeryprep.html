Case Study · 3 Months · UI/UX Designer

SurgeryPrep

A personalised mobile companion designed to guide patients through their surgical journey with clear, timely, and emotionally supportive information — reducing anxiety and clinical support burden.

Timeline
3 Months
Role
UI/UX Designer
Tools
Figma · Miro · UXPilot
Context
Regulated healthcare environment

The Problem

Patients forget 40–80% of medical information immediately after consultations. Printed instructions are easily misplaced and anxiety peaks in the days before surgery — leading to repeated calls to clinicians for basic reassurance. A regulated healthcare environment added further constraints around safety, privacy, and clinical liability.

01

Research & Insights

Conducted patient interviews across orthopedic, cardiac, and general surgery cohorts, plus interviews with 5 clinicians. Led journey mapping to document emotional highs and lows from diagnosis through recovery. Key finding: anxiety peaks 24–48 hours before surgery due to uncertainty — and post-surgery instructions are the most poorly retained.

02

Personas & Problem Framing

Created two primary personas: a cognitively overloaded patient motivated to "do everything right," and a time-constrained surgeon frustrated by repeated basic questions. This dual perspective kept design decisions anchored to both emotional reassurance and clinical efficiency throughout the project.

03

Ideation & IA

Ran Crazy 8s and explored 20+ concepts. Used a value vs. effort matrix to prioritise features. Excluded live clinician chat due to clinical risk, scalability, and regulatory complexity — instead focusing on progressive disclosure, personalised timelines, and smart FAQs. Designed a clear information architecture so users always knew where they were and what to do next.

04

Design & Prototype

Started with low-fidelity wireframes to validate core flows and hierarchy, then iterated into high-fidelity screens. Designed a calm, reassuring visual system using psychological principles to reduce anxiety. Every colour, typography, and interaction choice was grounded in user research rather than aesthetic preference.

Final Screens

— Dr. Emma Wilson, Director of Patient Experience, Regional Hospital Network
